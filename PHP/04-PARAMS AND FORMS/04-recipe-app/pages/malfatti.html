Malfatti (Italian Spinach Ricotta Dumplings)

Rustic spinach and ricotta dumplings from Tuscany, known as “malfatti” — meaning “badly formed” — for their charmingly rough shape. Soft, pillowy, and full of flavour, these “naked ravioli” are bound with egg and flour and often served with Napoli tomato sauce or brown butter and sage.

Prep: ~25 min Cook: ~10 min Yield: 4 servings Cuisine: Italian (Tuscany)

What Are Malfatti?

Malfatti are traditional Italian dumplings made of spinach and ricotta, bound with egg and a little flour, flavoured with parmesan and basil. The name means “badly formed”, referring to their rustic, uneven shape. In some regions, they’re called gnudi (“naked”) because they resemble ravioli without the pasta wrapper.

These tender dumplings are usually served with a tomato Napoli sauce or sometimes with brown butter and sage.

Ingredients

  • Ricotta (dry and crumbly is best) – the star ingredient. Avoid creamy ricotta; drain it if too wet.
  • Spinach – fresh or thawed frozen, finely chopped and well-drained.
  • Garlic & eschalot (shallot) – for aromatic sweetness. Substitute with ½ a red onion if needed.
  • Parmesan – for saltiness, richness, and structure.
  • Flour – about ½ cup, just enough to bind the mixture.
  • Egg – 1 whole + 1 yolk to help the dumplings hold together and add richness.
  • Basil – a handful of fresh leaves for fragrance (skip if unavailable; dried basil lacks flavour).
  • Salt & Pepper – to season to taste.

Tip: If the ricotta mixture is too wet to shape, add a touch more flour until just firm enough to form dumplings.

Instructions

  1. Drain ricotta. Place in a sieve or wrap in a clean towel and let drain at least 30 minutes (longer if very wet).
  2. Sauté aromatics. In a pan with olive oil, cook garlic and eschalot until translucent. Let cool.
  3. Mix dumpling dough. In a bowl, combine ricotta, spinach, cooled aromatics, Parmesan, basil, egg and yolk, and flour. Season with salt and pepper. The mixture should be soft but shapeable.
  4. Shape. Using floured hands or spoons, form small ovals or balls (about 1½ inches). Place on a floured tray.
  5. Cook. Bring salted water to a gentle simmer (not a rolling boil). Cook in batches until dumplings float, about 2–3 minutes.
  6. Serve. Gently lift out with a slotted spoon. Serve with Napoli sauce or browned butter and sage, topped with more Parmesan.

Extra Tips

  • If ricotta is spreadable like butter, it’s too wet — drain before using.
  • You can freeze shaped raw Malfatti on a tray, then store in freezer bags. Cook from frozen.
  • Do not overmix; handle gently to keep them light.
  • They pair beautifully with a crisp white wine or light red.
Note: Malfatti are “imperfect” by design — rustic, soft, and comforting. Their charm lies in their simplicity.
